Spreading Points Evenly

A research walkthrough about turning irrational numbers into high-dimensional point sets that avoid clumps, gaps, and hidden bias.

The Question

How do you scatter dots in a box as evenly as possible?

Imagine I hand you a square and ask you to mark a hundred points inside it. The points should cover the square evenly — no big empty regions, no clusters. Easy enough by eye. But what if I ask you to do it in a cube? In a 5-dimensional cube? With a hundred million points? Now your eye is useless and you need a recipe.

This is not a toy puzzle. Whenever a computer estimates the area under a curve, simulates a physical system, prices an option, or renders light bouncing in a video game, it secretly relies on a cloud of well-spread points. The more evenly the points cover the space, the more accurate the answer. So the question becomes: what is the best recipe for spreading points?

Random points look scattered, but look closely and you'll find clumps and gaps. Random isn't even — random is random. A 200-year-old idea called a Kronecker sequence uses a very simple recipe to fill a square with almost eerie regularity. My research is about understanding why, how well, and how to make it work in higher dimensions.

The Three Key Ideas

1. The recipe: pick an irrational number

To make a Kronecker sequence in 1D, you pick an irrational number — call it α — and your nth point is just the fractional part of n·α. So if α = 0.618… (the golden ratio minus one), the points are at 0.618, 0.236, 0.854, 0.472, 0.090, 0.708, … and so on. They march around the unit interval, never repeating, never landing on top of each other, gradually filling every gap.

For higher dimensions you pick a vector of irrationals (α₁, α₂, …, α_d). Each coordinate gets its own irrational. Same recipe, more dimensions.

2. Some irrationals are "more irrational" than others

This sounds odd. Aren't all irrational numbers equally irrational? Mathematically: no. The golden ratio is, in a precise sense, the hardest irrational to approximate by fractions. It is the most stubborn. And that stubbornness is exactly what makes its Kronecker sequence beautifully spread.

Numbers like e and π are also irrational, but they have occasional good rational approximations (this is why π ≈ 22/7 is famous). Those good approximations cause the Kronecker sequence to briefly clump, then escape, then clump again. Stubborn irrationals don't clump.

3. We need a score: the Zaremba index ρ

Eyeballing evenness doesn't scale. We need a number. The number mathematicians have agreed on is called the Zaremba index, written ρ:

  • ρ near 1 — terrible. The points are basically aligned on a thin line.
  • ρ growing with N — good. The more points you add, the more evenly they spread.
  • ρ ≈ N — excellent. As good as theoretically possible.

Formally:

ρ(α, N) = min over nonzero g of (|g₁| · |g₂| · … · |g_d|)
          such that g · (Nα) ≡ 0 (mod N)

So the entire research project comes down to one question: given a dimension d and a number of points N, which generators α make ρ as big as possible — and how big can ρ even get?

Phase 1 — Building the calculator

Before you can study a thing, you must measure it. There are three different algorithms in the published literature, each from a different paper. I implemented all three and tested them on numbers I trusted: the golden ratio, √2, e. They all agreed. Foundation established.

Along the way I noticed the algorithms have a search bound called M, conventionally set to N. In practice, almost every interesting generator gets the right answer with M = N/4 — a 4× speedup that compounded across billions of later computations.

Phase 2 — One Dimension

I ran ρ for N from 1 to 1000, with several famous α values:

  • Golden ratio: ρ grew in a clean straight line with N. Perfect.
  • √2: also a clean straight line. Just as good.
  • e: chaotic. ρ would jump up, fall back, jump again.
  • π: same chaos as e.
Inside the calculator, ρ is the smallest member of a special list called the worst vectors. For the golden ratio, that worst vector locks at (1, 1) and never changes. For e and π, it jumps around, which is exactly why ρ jumps.

Fibonacci resonance

When N happens to be a Fibonacci number (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34, …) the golden ratio's ρ exhibits a phase transition — a sudden jump. And the worst vector at those N values? Often itself a Fibonacci number. The Fibonacci sequence haunts the golden ratio's behavior at every level. This is unique to the golden ratio and its quadratic-irrational cousins; transcendentals like π don't have this fingerprint.

Phase 3 — Going Higher

The golden ratio's higher-dimensional cousin is called the Plastic Number (ψ ≈ 1.3247…). It has the same "most irrational" property in 3D that the golden ratio has in 1D. I used it to build 3D, 4D, and 5D Kronecker sequences and measure them.

I also tested a famous theorem. Dick and Zaremba proved that for any generator in d dimensions:

ρ ≥ c · N / (log N)^(d−1)

I ran tens of thousands of random 3D generators and counted how many actually hit this bound.

99.98% of generators easily exceed the bound. It's mathematically true, but useless as a quality filter — almost everything passes.

Phase 4 — The Landscape

For each N from 23 to 1601, I computed ρ for thousands of 3D generators and looked at the full distribution. Three findings:

  • The median is a clean curve. The middle-of-the-pack generator scores ρ ≈ 0.756 · N / (log N)². That coefficient 0.756 is remarkably stable.
  • The worst case is always 1. Degenerate generators (like (1, 1, 1)) always exist. You can't just pick a random generator and pray.
  • The best case grows much faster than the median, and the gap widens with N. Finding a top-quality generator becomes statistically rarer as you scale up. You have to search.

Phase 5 — Pushing into 4D and 5D

Python could handle 3D up to N ≈ 1600. For higher dimensions I needed new machinery. The work to compute ρ grows roughly as N^d. At 5D and N = 1000, that's a quadrillion operations per generator times tens of thousands of generators. So I rebuilt the core in three steps:

1. Numba JIT for 4D

Numba compiles Python to native machine code at runtime. With a careful branch-and-bound search, the 4D inner loop ran ~200× faster than vanilla Python. N up to 2000 became reachable.

2. Lattice reduction (LLL) to prune the search

Before searching, I run LLL to find a "nicer" basis for the lattice — one where short vectors are easy to find. With a better basis, branch-and-bound prunes far more aggressively. A free preprocessing step that makes everything downstream faster.

3. C with OpenMP for 5D

For 5D even Numba wasn't enough. I rewrote the kernel in C, parallelized across all CPU cores with OpenMP, and used streaming I/O so memory stays bounded. The biggest 5D dataset (N = 1009) is 18 GB.

  • 4D distribution data up to N = 2003.
  • 5D distribution data up to N = 1009 — to my knowledge, the largest dataset of its kind.
  • The N / (log N)^(d−1) median pattern continues to hold in higher dimensions.

Why this matters

Quasi-Monte Carlo sequences power a remarkable amount of modern computation: physics simulations, financial Monte Carlo, light transport in 3D rendering, machine-learning sample generation. Every one of those applications quietly depends on a generator whose ρ is large. If you don't know your ρ, you don't know how accurate your simulation can ever be.

What did this research add?

  • Confirmed that the classical "noble" irrationals (golden ratio, Plastic Number) really are exceptional, and identified the Fibonacci-resonance fingerprint that explains why.
  • Demystified the celebrated Dick–Zaremba lower bound: it's true but trivially satisfied by 99.98% of generators.
  • Mapped the typical-quality landscape in 3D — median ≈ 0.756 · N / (log N)² — turning vague intuition into a concrete formula.
  • Pushed the computational frontier into 5D, generating the first large-scale distribution data at that dimension.
The story arc: an old, simple idea (Kronecker's sequence), measured by a 70-year-old quality score (Zaremba's index), studied in the high-dimensional regime that modern applications actually live in.

If you've read this far without a math background and you now have a rough mental picture of what was done and why — that was the entire goal.
